What Is Serial Filer in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Serial Filer

A serial filer is a debtor who files multiple bankruptcy cases within a short period, often to repeatedly invoke the automatic stay to delay creditors. Serial filers face increasingly severe consequences: a limited 30-day stay on the second filing within a year, and potentially no stay at all on the third filing.

How It Works

BAPCPA addressed serial filing abuse through Section 362(c)(3) and (c)(4). If a debtor files a second case within one year after a prior case was dismissed, the automatic stay expires after 30 days unless the debtor proves the new filing is in good faith. If a debtor files a third (or subsequent) case within one year after two or more dismissed cases, no automatic stay takes effect at all.

Serial filing also affects discharge eligibility. Under Section 727(a)(8) and (9), debtors must wait specific periods between discharge-eligible filings: 8 years between Chapter 7 discharges, 6 years between a Chapter 13 and Chapter 7, 4 years between a Chapter 7 and Chapter 13, and 2 years between Chapter 13 discharges.

Courts scrutinize serial filers for good faith under Section 1325(a)(7) in Chapter 13 cases. A pattern of filing and dismissing cases without completing a plan raises a strong inference of bad faith and can lead to denial of confirmation, dismissal with a bar to refiling for 180 days, or even sanctions.

Key Facts

  • Second filing within 1 year: stay limited to 30 days (Section 362(c)(3))
  • Third filing within 1 year: no automatic stay at all (Section 362(c)(4))
  • Discharge timing limits: 8 years (Ch.7-Ch.7), 4 years (Ch.7-Ch.13), 2 years (Ch.13-Ch.13)
  • Courts can bar refiling for 180 days after dismissal
  • 1328(f) governs repeat discharge eligibility in Chapter 13

Frequently Asked Questions

How long do I have to wait to file again after dismissal?

There is no mandatory waiting period, but filing within one year of a dismissal limits your automatic stay to 30 days (or eliminates it entirely if two prior dismissals exist). Practically, waiting at least one year avoids these stay limitations.

Can I get a discharge in back-to-back cases?

Timing limits apply: 8 years between Chapter 7 discharges, 4 years from Chapter 7 to Chapter 13, 2 years between Chapter 13 discharges. If you file too soon, you will not receive a discharge even if you complete the case.

What is a Section 109(g) bar?

Section 109(g) bars a debtor from filing for 180 days if the prior case was dismissed for willful failure to obey court orders or was voluntarily dismissed after a creditor sought relief from the stay. This is an absolute bar -- no exception.
