What Is Liquidation in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Liquidation

Liquidation in bankruptcy refers to the sale of the debtor's non-exempt property by the trustee to pay creditors. Chapter 7 is known as a liquidation bankruptcy because the trustee's primary role is to convert assets into cash for distribution. In practice, most Chapter 7 cases are 'no-asset' cases where nothing is liquidated.

How It Works

In Chapter 7, the trustee reviews the debtor's schedules and investigates whether any non-exempt assets exist. If the trustee identifies property worth selling, they file a notice of intent to sell and conduct the sale. The proceeds are distributed to creditors in the order of priority established by the Bankruptcy Code.

Distribution follows a strict hierarchy: secured creditors are paid from their collateral, then administrative expenses (trustee fees, attorney fees), then domestic support obligations, then other priority claims (taxes, wages), and finally general unsecured creditors. If funds are insufficient to pay all claims in a class, distribution is pro rata within that class.

Approximately 95% of Chapter 7 cases are no-asset cases, meaning the trustee finds no property worth liquidating after exemptions. In these cases, the trustee files a report of no distribution and the case proceeds to discharge without any property being sold.

Key Facts

  • Chapter 7 is called 'liquidation' bankruptcy
  • About 95% of Chapter 7 cases are no-asset -- nothing is sold
  • Distribution order: secured -> administrative -> priority -> general unsecured
  • The trustee handles all sales and distributions
  • Exempt property is not subject to liquidation

Frequently Asked Questions

Will the trustee sell my belongings?

In most cases, no. Personal belongings (clothing, furniture, appliances) are almost always fully exempt. The trustee targets high-value non-exempt assets: non-exempt home equity, valuable vehicles, investment accounts, and tax refunds.

What is a no-asset case?

A no-asset case is a Chapter 7 case where the trustee determines there are no non-exempt assets worth selling. Approximately 95% of consumer Chapter 7 cases are no-asset. Creditors do not need to file proofs of claim in no-asset cases.

Can I buy back property from the trustee?

Sometimes. If the trustee is selling property, you may be able to purchase it back at the sale. Some trustees will negotiate a buyback if you can pay the non-exempt equity amount. This can be useful for sentimental items or property with value to you beyond its market price.
