What Is Voluntary Petition in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Voluntary Petition

A voluntary petition is a bankruptcy case initiated by the debtor, as opposed to an involuntary petition filed by creditors. The vast majority of bankruptcy cases -- over 99% -- are voluntary. The debtor chooses when to file, which chapter to file under, and which court to file in.

How It Works

Filing a voluntary petition is the first step in any self-initiated bankruptcy case. The debtor prepares the petition (Official Form 101), bankruptcy schedules, statement of financial affairs, means test form, and other required documents. The petition is filed with the clerk of the bankruptcy court in the district where the debtor has lived for the majority of the prior 180 days.

Before filing, the debtor must complete a credit counseling course from an approved provider within 180 days of filing. The certificate of completion must be filed with the petition. The debtor must also pay the filing fee ($338 for Chapter 7, $313 for Chapter 13) or file an application for installment payments or fee waiver.

The moment the voluntary petition is filed, three things happen: the automatic stay takes effect (stopping all collection activity), the bankruptcy estate is created (encompassing all of the debtor's property), and the case is assigned a number and trustee. Individual debtors can file singly or jointly with a spouse.

Key Facts

  • Over 99% of bankruptcy cases are voluntary -- filed by the debtor
  • Requires pre-filing credit counseling certificate
  • Can be filed individually or jointly with a spouse
  • Triggers the automatic stay and creates the bankruptcy estate immediately
  • The debtor chooses the chapter (7, 11, 12, or 13)

Frequently Asked Questions

Can my spouse and I file together?

Yes. Married couples can file a joint voluntary petition, which creates a single bankruptcy estate and a single case. This is often more efficient and less expensive than filing two separate cases.

Where do I file?

You file in the federal bankruptcy court for the district where you have lived (or had your principal place of business) for the majority of the 180 days before filing. If you recently moved, you may need to file in your previous district.

Can I change chapters after filing?

Yes. You can convert a voluntary case from one chapter to another in most situations. For example, converting from Chapter 13 to Chapter 7 if you can no longer make plan payments, or from Chapter 7 to Chapter 13 if you want to keep property the trustee would sell.
