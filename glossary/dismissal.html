What Is Dismissal in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Dismissal

Dismissal in bankruptcy terminates the case without the debtor receiving a discharge. The automatic stay is lifted, creditors can resume collection, and the debtor's debts remain. Dismissal can be voluntary (requested by the debtor) or involuntary (ordered by the court for cause).

How It Works

A bankruptcy case can be dismissed for many reasons: the debtor fails to file required documents, misses the 341 meeting, fails to make Chapter 13 plan payments, fails the means test, or commits fraud. The debtor can also request voluntary dismissal -- in Chapter 13, the debtor has an absolute right to dismiss under Section 1307(b).

Dismissal generally restores the status quo as if the bankruptcy was never filed. Liens are reinstated, any transfers avoided by the trustee may be reversed, and creditors can resume collection. However, property sold by the trustee before dismissal may not be recoverable.

There are consequences to dismissal beyond losing bankruptcy protection. If the debtor refiles within one year, the automatic stay is limited to 30 days (for one prior dismissal) or eliminated entirely (for two or more prior dismissals within the year). These limitations are found in Section 362(c)(3) and (c)(4).

Key Facts

  • Terminates the case without a discharge -- debts remain
  • In Chapter 13, the debtor has an absolute right to voluntary dismissal
  • Common causes: failure to file documents, missed 341 meeting, failed means test
  • Refiling within 1 year limits the automatic stay to 30 days
  • Two dismissals within 1 year can eliminate the automatic stay entirely

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I refile after dismissal?

Yes, but there may be consequences. If you refile within one year, the automatic stay lasts only 30 days unless you prove the new case is filed in good faith. Two dismissals within a year can eliminate the stay entirely.

Is dismissal the same as denial of discharge?

No. Dismissal ends the case without a discharge, but the debtor can refile. Denial of discharge means the debtor went through the case but was denied a discharge -- typically for fraud or misconduct. The debts remain and refiling may not help.

Can I convert instead of having my case dismissed?

Often yes. In many situations, converting to a different chapter (e.g., Chapter 13 to Chapter 7) is preferable to dismissal because it preserves the automatic stay and may still result in a discharge.
