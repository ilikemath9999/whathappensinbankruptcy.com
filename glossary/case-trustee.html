What Is Case Trustee in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Case Trustee

A case trustee is a private individual appointed by the U.S. Trustee to administer a bankruptcy case. In Chapter 7, the trustee liquidates non-exempt assets and distributes proceeds to creditors. In Chapter 13, the trustee collects plan payments from the debtor and distributes them to creditors.

How It Works

Bankruptcy trustees are not government employees but private individuals who serve on panels maintained by the U.S. Trustee Program. Chapter 7 panel trustees are assigned cases on a rotating basis. Chapter 13 standing trustees handle all Chapter 13 cases in their assigned district or division.

In Chapter 7, the trustee reviews the debtor's schedules, conducts the 341 meeting, investigates the debtor's financial affairs, and liquidates any non-exempt property. Most Chapter 7 cases are 'no-asset' cases where the trustee finds nothing to distribute. The trustee is compensated from estate funds.

In Chapter 13, the standing trustee plays a different role: collecting the debtor's monthly plan payments and distributing them to creditors according to the confirmed plan. The Chapter 13 trustee also monitors plan compliance, reviews proposed plan modifications, and can move for dismissal or conversion if the debtor falls behind.

Key Facts

  • Appointed by the U.S. Trustee from a panel of private individuals
  • Chapter 7 trustees liquidate non-exempt assets and distribute proceeds
  • Chapter 13 standing trustees collect and distribute monthly plan payments
  • Trustees conduct the 341 meeting and investigate the debtor's financial affairs
  • Trustee compensation comes from estate funds (percentage-based in Chapter 7)

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the trustee my enemy?

No. The trustee is a neutral fiduciary. Their job is to administer the estate fairly for creditors' benefit, but they are not adversarial. In the vast majority of consumer cases, the trustee's role is routine and non-confrontational.

Can I choose my trustee?

No. In Chapter 7, trustees are assigned on a rotating basis. In Chapter 13, a standing trustee handles all cases in the district. You cannot select or change your assigned trustee.

What happens if the trustee finds hidden assets?

The trustee can seek to deny your discharge, revoke a discharge already granted, or pursue criminal referral for bankruptcy fraud. Hiding assets from the trustee is a federal crime.
