What Is Statement of Financial Affairs in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Statement of Financial Affairs

The Statement of Financial Affairs (SOFA) is a required bankruptcy document that provides a detailed history of the debtor's financial transactions, lawsuits, property transfers, business interests, and other financial activities over the past several years. It is filed under penalty of perjury alongside the bankruptcy schedules.

How It Works

The SOFA (Official Form 107) asks approximately 30 questions about the debtor's recent financial history. Key questions cover: income from all sources for the current and prior two years, payments to creditors over $600 within 90 days (1 year for insiders), lawsuits the debtor was party to, property transfers within two years, gifts over $600, losses from fire/theft/gambling, and payments to attorneys.

The SOFA is one of the most important documents in the bankruptcy case. The trustee uses it to identify potential avoidance actions (preferences, fraudulent transfers), undisclosed assets, and potential grounds for denying discharge. Questions about payments to insiders and property transfers are particularly scrutinized.

Accuracy is essential. The SOFA is signed under penalty of perjury. Omissions or false statements can result in denial of discharge, revocation of a previously granted discharge, or criminal prosecution for bankruptcy fraud under 18 U.S.C. Section 152.

Key Facts

  • Official Form 107 -- approximately 30 questions about financial history
  • Filed under penalty of perjury with the bankruptcy schedules
  • Covers payments, transfers, lawsuits, income, and business interests
  • The trustee uses it to identify preferences, fraudulent transfers, and fraud
  • False statements can result in criminal prosecution

Frequently Asked Questions

How far back does the SOFA look?

Most questions cover 1-2 years before filing. Income questions cover the current and prior 2 calendar years. Payment to creditors questions cover 90 days (1 year for insiders). Property transfer questions cover 2 years. Some questions (lawsuits, financial accounts) have longer lookback periods.

What if I cannot remember every payment?

Review bank statements, credit card records, and check registers to compile accurate answers. If you truly cannot determine a transaction, disclose what you know and note the uncertainty. Incomplete disclosure is always better than false or no disclosure.

Is the SOFA public?

Yes. Like all bankruptcy filings, the SOFA is a public record available through PACER. Financial information, payments to creditors, and lawsuit history are all visible to anyone who accesses the filing.
