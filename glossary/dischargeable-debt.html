What Is Dischargeable Debt in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Dischargeable Debt

A dischargeable debt is any debt that can be permanently eliminated through bankruptcy. Most unsecured consumer debts are dischargeable, including credit card balances, medical bills, personal loans, past-due utility bills, and many types of lawsuit judgments.

How It Works

Bankruptcy law presumes that debts are dischargeable unless they fall into one of the specific exceptions listed in 11 U.S.C. Section 523(a). This means the burden is on the creditor to prove that a debt should survive the discharge, not on the debtor to prove it should be eliminated.

Common dischargeable debts include: credit card debt, medical bills, personal loans, payday loans, past-due rent, broken leases, business debts, deficiency balances after repossession, most lawsuit judgments, and obligations under contracts the debtor rejects in bankruptcy.

Whether a specific debt is dischargeable can depend on the facts. For example, credit card charges are normally dischargeable, but charges incurred through fraud (misrepresenting income on the application) or luxury purchases over $725 within 90 days of filing may be challenged as non-dischargeable.

Key Facts

  • Most unsecured consumer debts are presumed dischargeable
  • The burden is on the creditor to prove a debt is non-dischargeable
  • Credit card debt, medical bills, and personal loans are almost always dischargeable
  • Debts from fraud or willful injury require an adversary proceeding to be declared non-dischargeable
  • Chapter 13 historically discharged more debts than Chapter 7 (the 'super discharge')

Frequently Asked Questions

Are medical bills dischargeable?

Yes. Medical debt is one of the most commonly discharged types of debt in bankruptcy. It is general unsecured debt with no exceptions under Section 523.

Can I discharge payday loans?

Yes. Payday loans are unsecured consumer debts and are fully dischargeable. Some payday lenders attempt to claim fraud, but these challenges rarely succeed.

Are business debts dischargeable?

Yes. Business debts, including guarantees on business loans, are dischargeable. The means test does not even apply if the majority of the debtor's debts are business debts.
