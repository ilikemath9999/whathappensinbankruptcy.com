What Is Reaffirmation Agreement in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Reaffirmation Agreement

A reaffirmation agreement is a legally binding contract between the debtor and a creditor in which the debtor agrees to remain personally liable for a specific debt despite the bankruptcy discharge. They are most commonly used to keep a vehicle, allowing the debtor to continue making payments and retain the car.

How It Works

Under 11 U.S.C. Section 524(c), a debtor can reaffirm a debt by entering into a written agreement with the creditor before the discharge is entered. The agreement must disclose the amount of the debt, the interest rate, and a comparison of the reaffirmation to the debtor's budget. The debtor has 60 days after filing the agreement to rescind it.

If the debtor is represented by an attorney, the attorney must certify that the reaffirmation does not impose an undue hardship and that the debtor was fully advised. If the debtor is not represented, or if the debtor's budget shows a negative cash flow, the court must hold a hearing and determine whether the reaffirmation is in the debtor's best interest.

Reaffirmation carries significant risk: if the debtor later defaults on the reaffirmed debt, the creditor can pursue collection on the full balance as if the bankruptcy never happened. The debtor loses the protection of the discharge for that specific debt. For this reason, many bankruptcy attorneys advise against reaffirmation when the debtor is underwater on the loan.

Key Facts

  • Governed by 11 U.S.C. Section 524(c) and (d)
  • Must be filed before the discharge order is entered
  • The debtor can rescind within 60 days of filing the agreement
  • Attorney must certify no undue hardship if debtor is represented
  • If debtor defaults, full collection rights are restored as if no bankruptcy occurred

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I have to reaffirm my car loan?

No. Many debtors choose to keep paying without reaffirming (called 'retain and pay' or 'ride through'). Some lenders will continue reporting payments; others will not. Some circuits require debtors to choose: reaffirm, redeem, or surrender.

What happens if I reaffirm and then cannot pay?

The creditor can repossess the vehicle and sue you for the deficiency balance. You lose the bankruptcy discharge protection for that debt. This is the primary risk of reaffirmation.

Can I reaffirm a credit card?

Technically yes, but this is almost never advisable. Credit card debt is unsecured and dischargeable. Reaffirming it gives up the benefit of the discharge without any corresponding benefit to the debtor.
