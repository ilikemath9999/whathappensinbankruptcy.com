What Is Cram Down in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Cram Down

A cram down allows a bankruptcy court to confirm a reorganization plan over the objection of one or more classes of creditors, provided the plan meets specific statutory requirements. In Chapter 13, it also refers to reducing a secured claim to the collateral's current value.

How It Works

In Chapter 13, the term 'cram down' commonly refers to reducing the amount owed on a secured debt to the current value of the collateral. If a debtor owes $15,000 on a car worth $9,000, the court can 'cram down' the secured claim to $9,000, with the remaining $6,000 treated as unsecured debt. This requires that the loan be more than 910 days old for vehicles (the 'hanging paragraph').

In Chapter 11, cram down is the mechanism for confirming a plan over the objection of a dissenting class of creditors under Section 1129(b). The plan must be 'fair and equitable' and must not 'discriminate unfairly' against the dissenting class. For secured creditors, fair and equitable means retaining liens and receiving the present value of the collateral.

The interest rate applied in a cram down is determined by the 'prime plus' approach established in Till v. SCS Credit Corp. (2004). The court starts with the prime rate and adds a risk adjustment, typically 1-3%, based on the circumstances of the case.

Key Facts

  • Chapter 13 vehicle cram down requires the loan to be more than 910 days old
  • Reduces secured claim to current value of collateral
  • Chapter 11 cram down requires the plan to be 'fair and equitable'
  • Interest rate set using 'prime plus' approach (Till v. SCS Credit Corp.)
  • The 'hanging paragraph' (910-day rule) prevents cram down on recent vehicle loans

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I cram down my mortgage?

Generally no. Section 1322(b)(2) prohibits modifying a mortgage secured only by the debtor's principal residence. However, if the mortgage also secures other property, modification may be possible. Second mortgages can be stripped in some circuits if the home is underwater.

What is the 910-day rule?

The 910-day rule (BAPCPA's 'hanging paragraph') prevents cram down on vehicle loans incurred within 910 days (about 2.5 years) before filing. For these recent loans, the debtor must pay the full contract balance, not just the vehicle's current value.

How is the cram down interest rate calculated?

Courts typically use the 'prime plus' formula from Till v. SCS Credit Corp.: start with the national prime rate and add 1-3% for risk. The total rate is usually lower than the original contract rate.
