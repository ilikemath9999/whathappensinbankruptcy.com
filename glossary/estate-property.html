What Is Estate Property in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Estate Property

Estate property is any property that becomes part of the bankruptcy estate when the case is filed. Under 11 U.S.C. Section 541, it includes virtually everything the debtor owns or has an interest in as of the filing date -- real estate, personal property, bank accounts, investments, lawsuits, and even intangible rights.

How It Works

The scope of estate property is intentionally broad. Section 541(a)(1) includes 'all legal and equitable interests of the debtor in property as of the commencement of the case.' This catches tangible property (house, car, furniture), financial assets (bank accounts, stocks, tax refunds), and intangible interests (pending lawsuits, intellectual property, contract rights).

Certain property is excluded from the estate: most ERISA-qualified retirement accounts (401(k), pension, IRA), education savings accounts (529 plans), and property held in certain trusts. These exclusions operate differently from exemptions -- excluded property never enters the estate at all.

Once property enters the estate, the debtor must claim exemptions to protect it from liquidation. Any non-exempt estate property can be sold by the trustee in Chapter 7. In Chapter 13, estate property is not sold, but the plan must pay unsecured creditors at least as much as the non-exempt property is worth (the best interests test).

Key Facts

  • Defined broadly by 11 U.S.C. Section 541 to include almost everything
  • Includes tangible property, financial assets, lawsuits, and intangible rights
  • ERISA retirement accounts are excluded (not just exempt) from the estate
  • In Chapter 13, post-filing earnings also become estate property
  • The debtor claims exemptions to protect estate property from liquidation

Frequently Asked Questions

Does my tax refund become estate property?

Yes. Tax refunds for periods before the filing date are estate property. If you are expecting a large refund, consult with your attorney about timing. Some districts allow debtors to exempt a portion of the refund.

What about property I co-own with someone else?

Your interest in co-owned property enters the estate. In Chapter 7, the trustee can sell the property (including a co-owner's interest in some circumstances) but must protect the co-owner's share of the proceeds.

Is my engagement ring estate property?

Yes, but it is almost certainly protected by your state's personal property exemption. The distinction is that it enters the estate and you must claim the exemption to keep it.
