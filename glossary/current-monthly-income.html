What Is Current Monthly Income in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Current Monthly Income

Current monthly income (CMI) is a defined term in bankruptcy law meaning the debtor's average monthly gross income from all sources over the six full calendar months before the bankruptcy filing date. It is used to determine whether the debtor is above or below median income for means test purposes.

How It Works

CMI is calculated by adding all gross income from all sources received during the six complete calendar months before filing, then dividing by six. For example, if filing in July, the calculation uses income from January through June. This includes wages, salary, commissions, bonuses, overtime, business income, rental income, pension, and contributions from others.

Certain income sources are excluded from CMI: Social Security benefits, payments to victims of war crimes or terrorism, and VA disability benefits. These exclusions can significantly help debtors who rely on these income sources qualify for Chapter 7.

CMI is reported on Form 122A-1 (Chapter 7) or Form 122C-1 (Chapter 13). The annualized CMI is compared to the state median for the debtor's household size. If below median, the debtor passes the means test. If above, the debtor must complete the full means test calculation.

Key Facts

  • Defined at 11 U.S.C. Section 101(10A)
  • Based on 6 full calendar months before the filing month (not the filing date)
  • Includes almost all income: wages, business, rental, pension, and third-party contributions
  • Social Security and VA disability benefits are excluded
  • Reported on Form 122A-1 (Chapter 7) or Form 122C-1 (Chapter 13)

Frequently Asked Questions

Why does bankruptcy use a 6-month average instead of current income?

Congress designed the means test to look at the debtor's recent earnings history, not just a single month's income. This prevents debtors from strategically timing their filing to take advantage of a temporarily low-income month.

Does my spouse's income count?

If you are married and filing individually, your spouse's income is included in the household CMI calculation on Form 122A-1 but can be offset by a 'marital adjustment' for the spouse's separate expenses on Form 122A-2.

Can I time my filing to lower my CMI?

Yes, strategic timing is common. If you received a large bonus, overtime, or settlement in a recent month, waiting until that month drops out of the 6-month window can lower your CMI and help you pass the means test.
