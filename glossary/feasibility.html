What Is Feasibility in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Feasibility

Feasibility in bankruptcy means the debtor's proposed repayment plan is realistic and the debtor can actually make all required payments. The court will not confirm a plan that the debtor is unlikely to complete. Feasibility is a mandatory requirement for plan confirmation in both Chapter 13 and Chapter 11.

How It Works

Under 11 U.S.C. Section 1325(a)(6), the court must find that 'the debtor will be able to make all payments under the plan and to comply with the plan.' This is more than a mathematical exercise -- the court considers the debtor's income stability, employment history, necessary expenses, and track record of making payments during the case.

The trustee frequently challenges feasibility when a debtor's budget is unrealistically tight -- for example, a plan requiring the debtor to live on $200/month for food or assuming zero transportation costs. The court may also question feasibility if the debtor has a history of plan failures in prior cases.

In Chapter 11, feasibility under Section 1129(a)(11) requires that confirmation is 'not likely to be followed by the liquidation, or the need for further financial reorganization, of the debtor.' This is sometimes called the 'visionary plan' test -- the plan must be grounded in reality, not wishful thinking.

Key Facts

  • Required for confirmation under Section 1325(a)(6) (Ch.13) and Section 1129(a)(11) (Ch.11)
  • The court evaluates whether the debtor can actually make all plan payments
  • Unrealistic budgets, unstable income, and prior failures undermine feasibility
  • The debtor bears the burden of proving feasibility
  • Post-confirmation payment history can trigger modification or dismissal

Frequently Asked Questions

What happens if my plan is not feasible?

The court will deny confirmation and give you an opportunity to propose a modified plan. If no feasible plan can be proposed, the case may be dismissed or converted to Chapter 7.

How does the court evaluate feasibility?

The court looks at your income, expenses, employment stability, and whether you have been making adequate protection payments during the case. If the numbers do not add up or your budget is unrealistically lean, the court will find the plan not feasible.

Can I modify a confirmed plan if I cannot make payments?

Yes. Plan modification under Section 1329 allows you to adjust payments if your circumstances change -- job loss, medical emergency, or divorce. The modified plan must still be feasible.
