What Is Chapter 7 in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Chapter 7

Chapter 7 is the most common form of bankruptcy, often called liquidation bankruptcy. The trustee sells the debtor's non-exempt property to pay creditors, and most remaining unsecured debts are discharged. The process typically takes 3 to 6 months from filing to discharge.

How It Works

Chapter 7 bankruptcy provides a fresh start by eliminating most unsecured debts -- credit cards, medical bills, personal loans, and old utility bills. The debtor must pass the means test (or have income below the state median) to qualify. After filing, the trustee reviews assets, conducts the 341 meeting, and determines whether any non-exempt property exists to liquidate.

In practice, approximately 95% of Chapter 7 cases are 'no-asset' cases, meaning the trustee finds no property worth liquidating. The debtor keeps all exempt property -- typically a home (up to the homestead exemption), a vehicle (up to the motor vehicle exemption), retirement accounts, and personal belongings.

The Chapter 7 discharge is typically entered about 60 days after the 341 meeting, roughly 4 months after filing. The discharge permanently eliminates the debtor's personal liability on dischargeable debts. Secured debts survive unless the debtor surrenders the collateral, redeems it, or reaffirms the debt.

Key Facts

  • Most common bankruptcy chapter -- over 60% of all consumer filings
  • Requires passing the means test or having below-median income
  • About 95% of cases are 'no-asset' -- the debtor keeps all property
  • Discharge entered approximately 60 days after the 341 meeting
  • Cannot file Chapter 7 again for 8 years after a prior Chapter 7 discharge

Frequently Asked Questions

What debts does Chapter 7 eliminate?

Chapter 7 eliminates most unsecured debts: credit cards, medical bills, personal loans, past-due utilities, and many lawsuit judgments. It does not eliminate student loans (usually), recent taxes, child support, alimony, or debts from fraud.

Will I lose my house in Chapter 7?

Not necessarily. If your home equity is within your state's homestead exemption and you stay current on mortgage payments, you can keep your home. The trustee will only sell your home if it has significant non-exempt equity.

How long does Chapter 7 stay on my credit report?

A Chapter 7 bankruptcy remains on your credit report for 10 years from the filing date. However, most people see significant credit score improvement within 12-18 months of discharge.
