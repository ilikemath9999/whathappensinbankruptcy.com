What Is Disposable Income in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Disposable Income

Disposable income in bankruptcy is the debtor's current monthly income minus reasonably necessary expenses as determined by the means test. In Chapter 13, all projected disposable income over the plan period must be committed to repaying unsecured creditors.

How It Works

Under 11 U.S.C. Section 1325(b), if the trustee or an unsecured creditor objects to confirmation of a Chapter 13 plan, the debtor must commit all projected disposable income to the plan. For above-median debtors, expenses are calculated using IRS standards and actual secured debt payments. For below-median debtors, actual reasonable expenses are used.

The disposable income calculation is performed on Form 122C-2 for above-median debtors. Allowed deductions include taxes, mandatory payroll deductions, health insurance, child care, housing, transportation, secured debt payments, priority debt payments, and other necessary expenses.

The 'projected disposable income' concept was clarified by the Supreme Court in Hamilton v. Lanning (2010), which held that courts should use a forward-looking approach. If the debtor's income is expected to change (job loss, raise, retirement), the court can adjust the calculation to reflect anticipated future income rather than relying solely on historical figures.

Key Facts

  • Defined at 11 U.S.C. Section 1325(b)(2)
  • Above-median debtors use IRS expense standards; below-median use actual expenses
  • All projected disposable income must go to unsecured creditors in Chapter 13
  • Courts use a forward-looking approach per Hamilton v. Lanning (2010)
  • Calculated on Form 122C-2 (Chapter 13 full means test)

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I keep any income for myself in Chapter 13?

Yes. Disposable income is what remains after deducting all reasonably necessary expenses. You keep enough to cover housing, food, transportation, utilities, insurance, and other essential living costs. Only the surplus goes to creditors.

What if my income changes during the plan?

You can seek a plan modification. If your income increases, the trustee may also seek modification to increase payments. The court will evaluate whether the modified plan meets the disposable income test based on current circumstances.

Do I have to use IRS expense standards?

Only if you are above median income. Below-median debtors use their actual reasonable expenses. Even above-median debtors can claim actual expenses for some categories, like health care costs exceeding the IRS standard.
