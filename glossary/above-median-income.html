What Is Above Median Income in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Above Median Income

Above median income in bankruptcy means the debtor's current monthly income, annualized, exceeds the median household income for their state and household size. Debtors above the median must pass the means test to qualify for Chapter 7 or must file under Chapter 13 with a five-year repayment plan.

How It Works

The Bankruptcy Abuse Prevention and Consumer Protection Act of 2005 (BAPCPA) introduced the means test to prevent abuse of Chapter 7 by debtors who can afford to repay some debt. The first step is comparing the debtor's annualized current monthly income to the state median for their household size.

If the debtor's income is below the median, they pass the means test automatically and can file Chapter 7. If above the median, they must complete the full means test calculation on Form 122A-2, which deducts allowed expenses to determine whether enough disposable income remains to fund a Chapter 13 plan.

Median income figures are published by the U.S. Census Bureau and updated periodically by the U.S. Trustee Program. The calculation uses the debtor's average income over the six full calendar months before filing -- not current income at the time of filing.

Key Facts

  • Based on the six-month average income before filing, not current income
  • Median figures vary by state and household size and are updated regularly
  • Above-median debtors must complete the full means test (Form 122A-2)
  • Above-median Chapter 13 debtors must commit to a 5-year plan (below-median: 3 years)
  • Social Security income is excluded from the means test calculation

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the median income for my state?

Median income varies by state and household size. The U.S. Trustee Program publishes current figures at justice.gov/ust. For a household of four, the national median is approximately $100,000-$115,000, but it ranges significantly by state.

Can I still file Chapter 7 if I am above median income?

Yes, if you pass the full means test. The means test allows deductions for secured debt payments, taxes, health insurance, and other necessary expenses. Many above-median debtors still qualify for Chapter 7.

What income counts toward the means test?

Nearly all income counts: wages, salary, business income, rental income, pension, disability (except Social Security and VA disability), unemployment, and contributions from others toward household expenses.
