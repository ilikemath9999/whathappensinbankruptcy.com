What Is Means Test in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Means Test

The means test is a formula that determines whether a debtor qualifies for Chapter 7 bankruptcy based on their income and expenses. Introduced by BAPCPA in 2005, it compares the debtor's income to the state median and calculates disposable income to prevent higher-income debtors from abusing Chapter 7.

How It Works

The means test has two stages. First, the debtor's annualized current monthly income is compared to the median income for their state and household size. If below the median, the debtor passes automatically and can file Chapter 7 without further calculation.

If above the median, the debtor must complete the full means test on Form 122A-2. This calculation deducts standardized expenses (using IRS Local and National Standards), actual secured debt payments, priority debt payments, and certain other allowed expenses from CMI. If the resulting disposable income is too high, the case is presumed abusive and may be dismissed or converted to Chapter 13.

The means test does not apply if the debtor's debts are primarily business debts (more than 50% of total debt is non-consumer). It also does not apply to disabled veterans whose debts were incurred primarily during active duty. Even if a debtor fails the means test, the presumption of abuse can be rebutted by showing special circumstances.

Key Facts

  • Introduced by BAPCPA in 2005 to prevent Chapter 7 abuse
  • Step 1: Compare income to state median for household size
  • Step 2 (above median only): Calculate disposable income using IRS standards
  • Does not apply to primarily business debt or disabled veterans
  • Presumption of abuse can be rebutted by 'special circumstances'

Frequently Asked Questions

What if I fail the means test?

You can file Chapter 13 instead, attempt to rebut the presumption of abuse with special circumstances (such as a medical condition or recently reduced income), or wait until your 6-month income average drops below the threshold.

Does the means test look at household expenses?

For above-median debtors, expenses are calculated using IRS standards (national and local), not actual expenses. You get the IRS-allowed amount for housing, food, transportation, and healthcare, plus actual payments on secured debts and priority claims.

Can I avoid the means test?

If your debts are primarily business debts (more than 50%), the means test does not apply. Otherwise, all Chapter 7 filers must complete at least the first part of the means test (income comparison).
