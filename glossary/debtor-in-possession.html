What Is Debtor in Possession in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Debtor in Possession

A debtor in possession (DIP) is a debtor in a Chapter 11 bankruptcy case who retains control of their assets and continues to operate their business under court supervision. The DIP has most of the powers and duties of a trustee, including the duty of loyalty to creditors.

How It Works

In Chapter 11, the debtor automatically becomes the debtor in possession when the case is filed. Unlike Chapter 7, no trustee is appointed to take over the business. The DIP continues to manage day-to-day operations, pay employees, serve customers, and run the business in the ordinary course.

The DIP has enhanced obligations compared to operating outside of bankruptcy. The DIP must file monthly operating reports with the court, maintain adequate insurance, pay post-petition taxes, and operate in the best interests of creditors. The DIP cannot make extraordinary transactions (selling assets outside the ordinary course, obtaining new financing) without court approval.

If the DIP engages in fraud, dishonesty, incompetence, or gross mismanagement, the court can appoint a Chapter 11 trustee to replace the DIP. This is relatively rare -- courts prefer to leave the debtor in control when possible, especially in small business cases under Subchapter V.

Key Facts

  • The debtor automatically becomes DIP upon Chapter 11 filing -- no appointment needed
  • Has most powers and duties of a trustee under 11 U.S.C. Section 1107
  • Must file monthly operating reports and maintain insurance
  • Can operate in the ordinary course without court approval
  • A Chapter 11 trustee replaces the DIP only for cause (fraud, mismanagement)

Frequently Asked Questions

Can the DIP borrow money?

Yes, but obtaining new financing (DIP financing) requires court approval under Section 364. DIP lenders often receive super-priority administrative expense status or liens on estate property, making DIP financing expensive.

What is the difference between a DIP and a trustee?

A DIP is the debtor managing its own affairs. A trustee is a court-appointed outsider who replaces management. In most Chapter 11 cases, the debtor remains as DIP. A trustee is appointed only in rare cases involving fraud or gross mismanagement.

Does the DIP concept apply in Chapter 13?

Not formally. Chapter 13 debtors keep possession of their property but do not hold the legal title of 'debtor in possession.' The concept is specific to Chapter 11.
