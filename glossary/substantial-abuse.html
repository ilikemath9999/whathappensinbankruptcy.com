What Is Substantial Abuse in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Substantial Abuse

Substantial abuse, now commonly referred to simply as 'abuse,' is the standard under 11 U.S.C. Section 707(b) for dismissing a Chapter 7 case when the debtor has the financial ability to repay a meaningful portion of their debts. BAPCPA replaced the 'substantial abuse' standard with a broader 'abuse' standard tied to the means test.

How It Works

Before BAPCPA (2005), Section 707(b) allowed dismissal only for 'substantial abuse' -- a higher bar that gave debtors more latitude. BAPCPA lowered this to simply 'abuse' and introduced the means test as a mechanical way to identify abuse. If the debtor's means test shows sufficient disposable income, a presumption of abuse arises.

The presumption of abuse can be triggered in two ways: (1) the means test calculation on Form 122A-2 shows disposable income exceeding the statutory threshold (enough to pay at least 25% of general unsecured claims or $9,075 over 5 years), or (2) the totality of the debtor's financial circumstances shows the filing is an abuse of Chapter 7.

The debtor can rebut the presumption of abuse by showing 'special circumstances' -- such as a serious medical condition, a call to active military duty, or other documented expenses that reduce disposable income below the threshold. The burden shifts to the debtor once the presumption arises.

Key Facts

  • BAPCPA (2005) changed 'substantial abuse' to 'abuse' -- a lower bar for dismissal
  • The means test creates a mechanical presumption of abuse based on income
  • Abuse can also be found under the totality of circumstances (Section 707(b)(3))
  • The U.S. Trustee or court can raise the issue; creditors cannot in consumer cases with primarily consumer debts
  • Special circumstances can rebut the presumption (medical, military, etc.)

Frequently Asked Questions

Who can raise the issue of abuse?

The U.S. Trustee, the case trustee, or the court can raise abuse sua sponte. In cases where consumer debts predominate, individual creditors generally cannot file a Section 707(b) motion.

What are 'special circumstances'?

Special circumstances are conditions that justify additional expenses or income adjustments not captured by the means test. Examples include serious medical conditions requiring ongoing treatment, mandatory military deployment, and caring for a chronically ill family member.

What happens if my case is dismissed for abuse?

The case is dismissed and your debts remain. You can file Chapter 13 instead, which allows repayment over 3-5 years. There is no waiting period to convert or refile under a different chapter, though the limited stay provisions may apply.
