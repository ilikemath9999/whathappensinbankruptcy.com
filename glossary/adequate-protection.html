What Is Adequate Protection in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Adequate Protection

Adequate protection is a legal safeguard in bankruptcy that protects a secured creditor's interest in collateral while the automatic stay prevents the creditor from repossessing or foreclosing. It ensures the value of the creditor's lien is preserved during the bankruptcy case.

How It Works

When a debtor files bankruptcy, the automatic stay prevents secured creditors from repossessing collateral. But the creditor's collateral may be depreciating -- a car loses value every month, for example. Adequate protection compensates for this loss of value under 11 U.S.C. Section 361.

Adequate protection can take several forms: periodic cash payments to offset depreciation, an additional or replacement lien on other property, or any other relief that gives the creditor the equivalent of its interest in the collateral. In Chapter 13 cases, adequate protection payments on car loans and mortgages often begin within 30 days of filing.

If a secured creditor believes it is not receiving adequate protection, it can file a motion for relief from the automatic stay. The debtor then bears the burden of proving adequate protection is being provided. If the court finds the creditor is not adequately protected, it may lift the stay and allow repossession or foreclosure.

Key Facts

  • Required by 11 U.S.C. Section 361 to protect secured creditors during the stay
  • Common forms: periodic payments, additional liens, or replacement liens
  • In Chapter 13, adequate protection payments on cars typically begin within 30 days
  • Failure to provide adequate protection can result in the stay being lifted
  • The debtor bears the burden of proving adequate protection

Frequently Asked Questions

What happens if I do not make adequate protection payments?

The secured creditor can file a motion for relief from the automatic stay. If granted, the creditor can repossess or foreclose on the collateral despite the bankruptcy.

How much are adequate protection payments?

The amount depends on the type of collateral and how quickly it depreciates. For car loans, adequate protection payments are typically similar to regular monthly payments. For mortgages, they usually equal the regular monthly mortgage payment.

Is adequate protection the same as a plan payment?

Not exactly. Adequate protection payments protect the creditor during the period before plan confirmation. After confirmation, plan payments replace adequate protection payments, though the amounts may be the same.
