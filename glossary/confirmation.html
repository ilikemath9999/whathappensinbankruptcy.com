What Is Confirmation in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Confirmation

Confirmation is the court's approval of a Chapter 11 or Chapter 13 repayment plan, making the plan legally binding on the debtor and all creditors. Once confirmed, the plan governs the rights and obligations of all parties for the duration of the case.

How It Works

In Chapter 13, the debtor proposes a plan and the court holds a confirmation hearing, typically 20-45 days after the 341 meeting. The court evaluates whether the plan meets all statutory requirements: good faith, best interests test, feasibility, payment of priority claims in full, and commitment of all disposable income (for above-median debtors).

Creditors and the trustee can object to confirmation. Common objections include: the plan is not feasible, the debtor is not committing all disposable income, the plan was not proposed in good faith, or the plan fails the best interests test. The debtor can modify the plan to address objections.

In Chapter 11, confirmation is more complex. The debtor files a disclosure statement and plan, creditors vote on the plan by class, and the court evaluates whether the plan meets the requirements of Section 1129. A plan can be confirmed consensually (all impaired classes accept) or through cram down (over the objection of some classes).

Key Facts

  • Makes the plan legally binding on the debtor and all creditors
  • Chapter 13 requirements: good faith, feasibility, best interests, disposable income
  • Chapter 11 requires creditor voting and a disclosure statement
  • The trustee and creditors can object to plan confirmation
  • Post-confirmation modifications are possible but require court approval

Frequently Asked Questions

What happens if my plan is not confirmed?

The court may allow you to modify the plan and try again. If the plan cannot be modified to meet requirements, the case may be dismissed or converted to Chapter 7.

Can the plan be changed after confirmation?

Yes. Both the debtor and the trustee can request a plan modification after confirmation if circumstances change -- job loss, medical emergency, or changed financial circumstances.

How long does confirmation take?

In Chapter 13, confirmation typically occurs within 2-3 months of filing. In Chapter 11, the process is longer -- often 6-18 months depending on the complexity of the case and creditor negotiations.
