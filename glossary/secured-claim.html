What Is Secured Claim in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Secured Claim

A secured claim is a creditor's claim that is backed by a lien on the debtor's property (collateral). The secured portion of the claim equals the value of the collateral or the amount of the debt, whichever is less. Common secured claims include mortgages, car loans, and home equity lines of credit.

How It Works

Under 11 U.S.C. Section 506(a), a claim is secured to the extent of the value of the collateral. If a creditor is owed $20,000 and the collateral is worth $15,000, the claim is bifurcated: $15,000 is a secured claim and $5,000 is an unsecured claim. This bifurcation is the basis for cram down in Chapter 13.

Secured creditors have significant rights in bankruptcy. The automatic stay prevents them from repossessing, but they can seek relief from the stay if not adequately protected. In Chapter 7, the debtor must choose to reaffirm, redeem, or surrender secured property. In Chapter 13, secured claims can be paid through the plan.

The interest rate on a secured claim in Chapter 13 is determined by the court using the 'prime plus' approach from Till v. SCS Credit Corp. (2004). The court starts with the prime rate and adds a risk factor, typically 1-3%. The debtor pays the secured claim amount plus this interest rate over the life of the plan.

Key Facts

  • Defined by 11 U.S.C. Section 506(a) -- secured to the value of the collateral
  • Claims exceeding collateral value are bifurcated: secured + unsecured portions
  • Secured creditors can seek relief from the automatic stay
  • In Chapter 7: reaffirm, redeem, or surrender
  • In Chapter 13: paid through the plan at court-determined interest rate

Frequently Asked Questions

What if my car is worth less than I owe?

The claim is bifurcated under Section 506(a). The secured claim equals the car's value, and the excess is an unsecured claim. In Chapter 13, you may be able to cram down the loan to the car's value (if the loan is more than 910 days old).

Does my mortgage become unsecured if I am underwater?

No. Mortgages on the debtor's principal residence are protected from modification by Section 1322(b)(2). However, a wholly unsecured second mortgage can be stripped in Chapter 13.

What interest rate does a secured claim earn in Chapter 13?

The court sets the rate using the Till formula: prime rate plus a risk adjustment (usually 1-3%). This is typically lower than the original contract rate.
