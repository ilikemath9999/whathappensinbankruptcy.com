What Is Chapter 13 in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Chapter 13

Chapter 13 is a reorganization bankruptcy for individuals with regular income, allowing them to repay some or all debts through a 3 to 5-year repayment plan. It lets debtors keep their property -- including homes and vehicles -- while catching up on past-due payments under court protection.

How It Works

Chapter 13 is designed for individuals who have regular income and want to keep their property while repaying debts over time. The debtor proposes a plan that pays priority debts in full (taxes, support obligations), cures mortgage and car loan arrearages, and pays unsecured creditors a percentage based on disposable income and the best interests test.

Plan length depends on income: below-median debtors commit to a 3-year plan (with court approval to extend to 5), while above-median debtors must commit to a 5-year plan. Monthly payments go to the Chapter 13 standing trustee, who distributes funds to creditors according to the confirmed plan.

Chapter 13 offers several advantages over Chapter 7: it can save a home from foreclosure by curing arrears over time, strip junior liens from underwater homes (in some circuits), and discharge certain debts that survive Chapter 7. The debtor must make all plan payments to receive a discharge.

Key Facts

  • Available only to individuals (not businesses) with regular income
  • Debt limits: $2,750,000 in total debt (as of 2024 BAPPA adjustments)
  • Plan length: 3 years (below median) or 5 years (above median)
  • The debtor keeps all property, including homes and vehicles
  • Filing fee is $313 -- lower than Chapter 7

Frequently Asked Questions

Can Chapter 13 save my house from foreclosure?

Yes. Chapter 13 is specifically designed to cure mortgage arrears over the life of the plan while you resume regular monthly payments. As long as you make all plan payments and current mortgage payments, the lender cannot foreclose.

What happens if I cannot make my Chapter 13 payments?

You can seek a plan modification to lower payments, convert to Chapter 7, or request a hardship discharge if you have paid a significant portion of the plan. If none of these options work, the case may be dismissed.

How much do I pay in Chapter 13?

Your payment depends on your disposable income (income minus allowed expenses), the best interests test (at least what creditors would get in Chapter 7), and the amount needed to cure secured debt arrears and pay priority claims in full.
