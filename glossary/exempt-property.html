What Is Exempt Property in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Exempt Property

Exempt property is property that the debtor is legally entitled to keep, free from creditors' claims, despite filing for bankruptcy. Exemptions are determined by state law (or federal bankruptcy exemptions in states that allow the choice) and typically protect a home, vehicle, retirement accounts, and personal belongings.

How It Works

When a debtor files bankruptcy, all property enters the bankruptcy estate. The debtor then claims exemptions on Schedule C to remove specific property from the trustee's reach. Each state has its own exemption statutes, and some states allow debtors to choose between state and federal bankruptcy exemptions.

Common exemptions include the homestead exemption (protecting equity in the debtor's primary residence), motor vehicle exemption, personal property exemptions (clothing, furniture, appliances), tools of the trade, and the wildcard exemption (protecting any property up to a dollar limit). Retirement accounts (401(k), IRA, pension) are protected by federal law regardless of state exemptions.

If the debtor claims an exemption and no party objects within 30 days, the exemption is allowed automatically. The trustee or a creditor can object to an exemption if the debtor is overclaiming -- for example, claiming a homestead exemption on a rental property or valuing property too low.

Key Facts

  • Determined by state law -- exemption amounts vary dramatically by state
  • Some states allow choosing between state and federal exemptions
  • Retirement accounts are protected by federal law (ERISA) in all states
  • Exemptions must be claimed on Schedule C within 14 days of the 341 meeting
  • Objections to exemptions must be filed within 30 days of the 341 meeting

Frequently Asked Questions

Which states have the best exemptions?

Texas and Florida have unlimited homestead exemptions, making them the most debtor-friendly states. Kansas, Iowa, and South Dakota also have unlimited homesteads. States with low exemptions include Maryland, New Jersey, and Virginia.

Can I use federal exemptions?

Only if your state allows it. About 20 states let debtors choose between state and federal exemptions. The remaining states require debtors to use state exemptions exclusively.

What if my property is worth more than the exemption?

The trustee can sell the property, pay you the exempt amount, and distribute the remaining proceeds to creditors. This is why accurate property valuations on your schedules matter.
