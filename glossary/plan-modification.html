What Is Plan Modification in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Plan Modification

Plan modification is a change to a confirmed Chapter 13 repayment plan, requested when the debtor's financial circumstances change during the 3 to 5-year plan period. Modifications can increase or decrease payments, extend the plan duration, or alter the treatment of specific creditors.

How It Works

Under 11 U.S.C. Section 1329, the debtor, trustee, or holder of an unsecured claim can request modification of a confirmed Chapter 13 plan. Modifications are common because financial circumstances frequently change over a multi-year plan -- job changes, medical emergencies, divorce, or increases in income.

A proposed modification must still meet all the requirements for initial confirmation: good faith, best interests test, feasibility, and commitment of disposable income. The court holds a hearing on the proposed modification and creditors can object. The plan can be extended but generally not beyond five years from the original first payment date.

Common modifications include: reducing payments due to job loss or income reduction, increasing payments due to income increase (often initiated by the trustee), adding newly filed claims, adjusting for changes in secured debt (paying off a car loan), and surrendering property that was originally being retained.

Key Facts

  • Governed by 11 U.S.C. Section 1329
  • Can be requested by the debtor, trustee, or unsecured creditor
  • Modified plan must meet all original confirmation requirements
  • Plan can be extended but generally not beyond 5 years from the first payment
  • Common trigger: job loss, medical emergency, or income change

Frequently Asked Questions

Can the trustee force me to increase my payments?

Yes. If your income increases during the plan, the trustee can file a motion to modify the plan to capture the additional disposable income. The court will evaluate whether modification is warranted based on the change in circumstances.

How many times can I modify my plan?

There is no statutory limit on the number of modifications. However, frequent modifications may raise concerns about feasibility and good faith. Each modification requires court approval.

Can I extend my plan beyond 5 years?

Generally no. Section 1329(c) limits the plan to 60 months from the date of the first payment. However, some courts have allowed extensions beyond 5 years in extraordinary circumstances -- this is an unsettled area of law.
