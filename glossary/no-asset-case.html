What Is No-Asset Case in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › No-Asset Case

A no-asset case is a Chapter 7 bankruptcy case in which the trustee determines there is no non-exempt property available to liquidate and distribute to creditors. Approximately 95% of consumer Chapter 7 cases are no-asset cases, meaning creditors receive nothing from the estate.

How It Works

After reviewing the debtor's schedules and conducting the 341 meeting, the Chapter 7 trustee evaluates whether any non-exempt assets exist. If all property is either exempt, subject to valid liens exceeding the property's value, or worth too little to justify the cost of sale, the trustee files a report of no distribution.

In a no-asset case, creditors are not required to file proofs of claim because there will be no distribution. The court sends a notice informing creditors that it appears to be a no-asset case. If the trustee later discovers assets, the case can be converted to an asset case and creditors will be given an opportunity to file claims.

No-asset cases proceed more quickly and simply than asset cases. There is no need for the trustee to sell property, resolve disputes over asset values, or make distributions. The case typically moves from the 341 meeting to discharge in about 60 days.

Key Facts

  • Approximately 95% of consumer Chapter 7 cases are no-asset
  • The trustee files a 'report of no distribution'
  • Creditors do not need to file proofs of claim in no-asset cases
  • The case can be converted to asset if the trustee later discovers property
  • No-asset cases typically result in faster discharge

Frequently Asked Questions

If it is a no-asset case, why did I need to list everything?

Federal law requires full disclosure of all assets regardless of whether they are exempt. Failure to list assets is bankruptcy fraud and can result in denial of discharge, case dismissal, or criminal prosecution.

Can a no-asset case become an asset case?

Yes. If the trustee discovers previously undisclosed property, a pending lawsuit with value, or a preference payment worth recovering, the case can be reopened as an asset case. Creditors will then be given notice and an opportunity to file claims.

Does no-asset mean creditors get nothing?

From the bankruptcy estate, yes -- creditors receive no distribution. However, creditors with non-dischargeable debts (student loans, certain taxes, support obligations) can still collect after the case closes.
