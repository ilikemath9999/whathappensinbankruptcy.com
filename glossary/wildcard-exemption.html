What Is Wildcard Exemption in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Wildcard Exemption

The wildcard exemption allows a debtor to protect any type of property up to a specified dollar amount, regardless of the property's category. It is especially valuable for protecting assets that do not fit neatly into other exemption categories -- cash, tax refunds, bank account balances, and miscellaneous personal property.

How It Works

The federal wildcard exemption under 11 U.S.C. Section 522(d)(5) allows a debtor to exempt up to $1,475 of any property, plus up to $13,950 of any unused portion of the homestead exemption. This means a renter who does not use the homestead exemption can protect up to $15,425 in any property using the wildcard.

State wildcard exemptions vary significantly. Some states (like California's System 1, Missouri, and Ohio) offer generous wildcards of $5,000 or more. Other states offer no wildcard at all. The availability depends on whether the debtor's state allows the federal exemption choice and what the state's own wildcard provides.

The wildcard is often stacked with other exemptions. For example, a debtor might exempt a car under the motor vehicle exemption up to its limit, then apply wildcard dollars to protect additional equity in the same car. It can also protect assets that have no specific exemption category, like a coin collection, cryptocurrency, or money in a checking account.

Key Facts

  • Federal wildcard: $1,475 + up to $13,950 unused homestead = $15,425 max
  • Protects any type of property regardless of category
  • State wildcard amounts vary from $0 to $25,000+
  • Especially useful for renters who do not use a homestead exemption
  • Can be stacked with other specific exemptions

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use the wildcard on my car?

Yes. If your car's equity exceeds your state's motor vehicle exemption, you can apply wildcard dollars to protect the additional equity. This is a common strategy for keeping vehicles with moderate equity.

What if my state has no wildcard exemption?

If your state requires use of state exemptions and offers no wildcard, you are limited to the specific exemption categories your state provides. Some states compensate with generous specific exemptions even without a wildcard.

Can married couples double the wildcard?

In a joint filing, each spouse can claim their own set of exemptions. If the federal wildcard is available, a married couple could potentially protect up to $30,850 in any property using combined wildcards.
