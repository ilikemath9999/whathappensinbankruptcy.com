What Is Discharge Injunction in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Discharge Injunction

The discharge injunction is a permanent court order under 11 U.S.C. Section 524(a) that prohibits any act to collect, recover, or offset a discharged debt. It protects the debtor's fresh start by making it illegal for creditors to pursue collection on eliminated debts after the bankruptcy case concludes.

How It Works

When the court enters a discharge order, the discharge injunction takes effect automatically. It prohibits creditors from initiating or continuing any action to collect a discharged debt, including lawsuits, phone calls, letters, wage garnishments, and credit reporting of the debt as currently owing.

The discharge injunction is permanent and applies to the debtor personally. However, it does not protect co-debtors (except in Chapter 13, where the co-debtor stay provides limited protection during the plan). It also does not prevent enforcement of valid liens -- a creditor can still foreclose on a house or repossess a car if payments are not made.

Violations of the discharge injunction can be addressed through a contempt motion. Courts can award actual damages, attorney fees, and in egregious cases, punitive damages. Some circuits treat discharge injunction violations under Section 524 while others apply Section 105(a) contempt powers.

Key Facts

  • Codified at 11 U.S.C. Section 524(a)
  • Prohibits any act to collect a discharged debt -- permanently
  • Violations can result in contempt, damages, and attorney fees
  • Does not remove liens -- only eliminates personal liability
  • Does not protect co-debtors (except the Chapter 13 co-debtor stay)

Frequently Asked Questions

What should I do if a creditor contacts me about a discharged debt?

Notify the creditor in writing that the debt was discharged, provide a copy of the discharge order, and demand they cease collection. If they continue, consult a bankruptcy attorney about filing a contempt motion -- you may be entitled to damages.

Does the discharge injunction stop credit reporting?

Yes. Creditors must report discharged debts as 'discharged in bankruptcy' with a zero balance. Reporting a discharged debt as owing or delinquent violates the discharge injunction.

How long does the discharge injunction last?

The discharge injunction is permanent. There is no expiration date. Even after the bankruptcy is removed from your credit report (10 years for Chapter 7, 7 years for Chapter 13), the discharge injunction remains in effect forever.
