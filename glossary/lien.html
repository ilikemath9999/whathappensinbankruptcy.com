What Is Lien in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Lien

A lien is a legal right or interest that a creditor has in the debtor's property as security for a debt. Common liens include mortgage liens on real estate, security interests in vehicles, judgment liens from lawsuits, and tax liens. Liens generally survive bankruptcy even when the underlying debt is discharged.

How It Works

Liens give creditors a secured interest in specific property. If the debtor does not pay, the lienholder can foreclose on real estate or repossess personal property to satisfy the debt. In bankruptcy, liens are treated differently from unsecured debts -- they attach to the property, not just the debtor personally.

When a debtor receives a discharge, the personal obligation to pay the debt is eliminated, but the lien remains on the property. This means a mortgage lender can still foreclose if payments are not made, even after discharge. To fully clear a lien, the debtor must either pay it, avoid it (if avoidable under Section 522(f)), strip it (if the property is underwater), or surrender the property.

There are three main types of liens: consensual liens (mortgages, car loans), judicial liens (from court judgments), and statutory liens (tax liens, mechanic's liens). Different types of liens have different vulnerability to avoidance in bankruptcy. Judicial liens on exempt property can be avoided under Section 522(f), while consensual and statutory liens generally cannot.

Key Facts

  • Liens survive bankruptcy discharge -- they attach to property, not the person
  • Three types: consensual (mortgage), judicial (judgment), and statutory (tax)
  • Judicial liens on exempt property can be avoided under Section 522(f)
  • The discharge eliminates personal liability but does not remove the lien
  • Liens are valued as secured claims up to the value of the collateral

Frequently Asked Questions

Does bankruptcy remove liens?

Generally no. Bankruptcy discharges personal liability but leaves liens intact. However, certain liens can be avoided (judicial liens on exempt property under Section 522(f)) or stripped (junior liens on underwater homes in Chapter 13).

What is a judgment lien?

A judgment lien is created when a creditor wins a lawsuit and records the judgment against the debtor's property (usually real estate). Unlike a mortgage, the debtor did not consent to this lien. Judgment liens on exempt property can often be avoided in bankruptcy.

What happens to my car lien in bankruptcy?

In Chapter 7, you must continue paying or surrender the vehicle. In Chapter 13, you can pay through the plan, and if the loan is more than 910 days old, you may be able to cram down the secured claim to the vehicle's current value.
