What Is Discharge in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Discharge

A discharge is a court order that permanently eliminates the debtor's personal liability for specified debts, prohibiting creditors from ever attempting to collect those debts. It is the primary goal of most individual bankruptcy filings and provides the 'fresh start' that bankruptcy law is designed to give.

How It Works

The discharge is the culmination of the bankruptcy process. In Chapter 7, the discharge is typically entered about 60 days after the 341 meeting -- roughly 4 months after filing. In Chapter 13, the discharge is entered after the debtor completes all plan payments, which takes 3-5 years.

The discharge operates as a permanent injunction under 11 U.S.C. Section 524(a). Any creditor who attempts to collect a discharged debt -- including phone calls, letters, lawsuits, or reporting the debt as owing on credit reports -- violates the discharge injunction and can be held in contempt of court.

Not all debts are dischargeable. Certain debts survive bankruptcy, including most student loans, recent tax obligations, child support and alimony, debts from fraud, DUI-related debts, and certain government fines. The categories of non-dischargeable debts are listed in 11 U.S.C. Section 523.

Key Facts

  • Permanently eliminates personal liability for dischargeable debts
  • Chapter 7 discharge: approximately 60 days after the 341 meeting
  • Chapter 13 discharge: after completing all plan payments (3-5 years)
  • Operates as a permanent injunction under Section 524(a)
  • Some debts (student loans, recent taxes, support obligations) survive discharge

Frequently Asked Questions

Does discharge eliminate liens?

No. A discharge eliminates personal liability but does not remove liens. A creditor with a mortgage lien can still foreclose if payments are not made, even though the debtor's personal liability on the note is discharged.

Can a discharge be revoked?

Yes, in limited circumstances. Under Section 727(d), a discharge can be revoked within one year if the debtor obtained it through fraud, failed to disclose property, or refused to obey a court order.

What is the difference between Chapter 7 and Chapter 13 discharge?

Chapter 13 historically offered a broader discharge (called a 'super discharge'), but BAPCPA narrowed this advantage significantly. The main differences now are timing (4 months vs. 3-5 years) and the fact that Chapter 13 can discharge some debts that survive Chapter 7.
