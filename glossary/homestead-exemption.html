What Is Homestead Exemption in Bankruptcy?

Home › Glossary › Homestead Exemption

The homestead exemption protects equity in the debtor's primary residence from being seized to pay creditors in bankruptcy. The amount of protection varies dramatically by state -- from as little as $5,000 in some states to unlimited in Texas, Florida, Kansas, Iowa, and South Dakota.

How It Works

The homestead exemption protects a specified dollar amount of equity in the debtor's primary residence. If the debtor's home equity is at or below the exemption limit, the trustee cannot sell the home. If equity exceeds the exemption, the trustee may sell the home but must pay the debtor the exempt amount from the sale proceeds.

State exemption amounts vary enormously. Texas and Florida offer unlimited homestead exemptions -- a debtor could have $10 million in home equity and it would be fully protected. At the other extreme, states like New Jersey and Maryland offer exemptions of only $25,000 or less. Some states allow married couples to double the exemption.

BAPCPA imposed a cap on homestead exemptions for recently purchased homes: if the debtor acquired the home within 1,215 days (about 3.3 years) of filing, the exemption is capped at $189,050 (adjusted periodically) regardless of state law. This prevents debtors from buying expensive homes in unlimited-exemption states right before filing.

Key Facts

  • Protects equity in the debtor's primary residence
  • Amounts vary by state: from $5,000 (some states) to unlimited (TX, FL, KS, IA, SD)
  • BAPCPA caps exemption at $189,050 for homes purchased within 1,215 days of filing
  • Only applies to the debtor's principal residence, not investment properties
  • Some states allow married couples to double (or 'stack') the exemption

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I keep my house in bankruptcy?

In most cases, yes. If your home equity is within your state's homestead exemption and you are current on mortgage payments, you keep your home. Even in states with low exemptions, many homeowners have little equity and are protected.

What counts as equity for the homestead exemption?

Equity is the fair market value of the home minus all mortgages, liens, and costs of sale. If your home is worth $250,000 and you owe $230,000, your equity is approximately $20,000 (minus hypothetical sale costs).

Can I exempt a mobile home?

In most states, yes, if it is your primary residence. Some states have separate mobile home exemptions. The homestead exemption generally applies to any dwelling the debtor uses as their primary residence.
